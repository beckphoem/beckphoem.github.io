Alike or Different?

Some brothers are alike,¹ and some are different. John and Larry are good examples. They’re both tall, and they look alike. But they like different things.

John is 27 years old. Larry is 25. John lives in New York City. He likes big cities. Larry doesn’t like big cities. He lives in Grove City, a small town in Pennsylvania.

Everyone needs friends. John has a lot of² friends but not many close friends. Larry doesn’t have a lot of friends, but he has a few³ very good friends. John likes people, and he loves parties. Larry likes people too, but he doesn’t like parties very much.

John likes languages. He speaks English, Spanish, and Arabic. Larry just speaks English.

John likes computers. He’s a computer programmer. Larry has a computer, but he doesn’t use it very much. He loves music, and he’s a music teacher.

John isn’t married, and he doesn’t want children. Larry is married. He and his wife don’t have children, but they want them.

John and Larry are very different, but they have fun together.

2 a lot of: a large number of

3 a few: a small number

STEP 2: GRAMMAR PRESENTATION

THE SIMPLE PRESENT: STATEMENTS

Affirmative & Negative Statements (I, You, We, They)

Affirmative Statements Negative Statements
Subject Verb Subject Do not / Does not Base Form of Verb
I / You / We / They like music. I / You / We / They do not (don't) like work.

Affirmative & Negative Statements (He, She, It)

Affirmative Statements Negative Statements
Subject Verb Subject Do not / Does not Base Form of Verb
He / She / Maria likes music. He / She / Maria does not (doesn't) like big cities.
It snows a lot here. It does not (doesn't) rain a lot in Los Angeles.

GRAMMAR NOTES

1. Uses of the Simple Present

Use the simple present to talk about facts and things that happen again and again.

  • I live in Redmond. (a fact)
  • Everyone needs food and water. (a fact)
  • He studies every evening. (a thing that happens again and again)

2. Affirmative Statements

In affirmative statements, use the base form of the verb with I, you, we, and they.

  • We want a big house.
  • They like their home.

The verb changes with he, she, and it:

  • Add -s to most verbs. (He needs help.)
  • Add -ies to verbs that end in a consonant + y. (She studies hard.)
  • Add -es to verbs that end in -ch, -o, -ss, -x, or -z. (Yukiko watches TV every evening. Jomo goes to the university every day.)

3. Negative Statements

In negative statements, use do not + the base form of the verb with I, you, we, and they.

  • They do not live in the city.

Use does not + the base form of the verb with he, she, and it.

  • She does not need help.

We often use the contractions don't and doesn't in speaking and informal writing.

4. Be and Have

Be and have are irregular verbs.

  • I am a student. Elena is a student, too.
  • I have a lot of friends. Bi-Yun has a lot of friends, too.
