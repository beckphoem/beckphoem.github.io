STEP 2: GRAMMAR PRESENTATION

COMPARATIVE ADJECTIVES

Comparative Adjectives with -er

The train is quicker than the bus.
The bus is cheaper.

Comparative Adjectives with More

Movies are more interesting than television.
Cars are more expensive than motorcycles.

Which / Who

Which more interesting, movies or TV shows?
Who is older, Ken or Laura?

GRAMMAR NOTES

1. Uses of Comparative Adjectives

Use the comparative form of an adjective + than to compare two people, places, or things.

Example: Ken is taller than Laura.

We can omit than if the meaning is clear.

Example: They're also cheaper. (cheaper than steak)

2. Comparatives of Short Adjectives

To form the comparative of short (one-syllable) adjectives, add -er.

young → younger

If the adjective ends in -e, just add -r.

large → larger

3. Comparatives of Two-Syllable Adjectives Ending in -y

To form the comparative, change the y to i and add -er.

easy → easier

tasty → tastier

4. Comparatives of Long Adjectives

To form the comparatives of most adjectives of two or more syllables, use more before the adjective.

crowded → more crowded

interesting → more interesting

5. Irregular Comparatives

The adjectives good, bad, and fun have irregular comparative forms.

  • good → better
  • bad → worse
  • fun → more fun

6. Which and Who

Use which to ask about a comparison of things or places.

A: Which is better, soda or juice? B: I think juice is better (than soda).

Use who to ask about people.

A: Who's older, you or your cousin? B: I am. I'm twenty-five, and he's twenty-three.
