I Have One Good Habit

JOSH: How’s it going, Steve? You look tired.

STEVE: Well, things are OK, but I am a little tired. I’m always tired. I only sleep about six hours a night.

JOSH: What time do you go to bed?

STEVE: I usually stay up late—until 12:30 or 1:00 a.m. And I get up at 6:30 or 7:00 a.m.

JOSH: Do you ever sleep late?

STEVE: Sometimes—on the weekends.

JOSH: Do you always eat lunch?

STEVE: Yeah. But I sometimes skip breakfast.

JOSH: So you don’t eat three meals a day?

STEVE: Rarely. I’m usually in a hurry in the morning.

JOSH: What do you eat for lunch and dinner?

STEVE: I always have a good dinner. But lunch . . . well, I’m always in a hurry then. So I usually go to a fast-food place near the university.

JOSH: Hmm. Not enough sleep. No breakfast. Fast food for lunch. Not good, my friend.

STEVE: Well, I have one good habit. I work out.

JOSH: Great. How often do you work out?

STEVE: Two or three times a year.

GRAMMAR NOTES

1. Definition: Adverbs of frequency say how often something happens. (e.g., I always skip breakfast.)

2. With Be: Adverbs of frequency come after the verb be. (e.g., I'm usually tired in the morning.)

3. With Other Verbs: Adverbs of frequency normally come before other verbs. (e.g., He usually goes to a fast-food place.)

4. Usually, Sometimes, Rarely, and Never: These can also come at the beginning or the end of a sentence. (e.g., Usually I get up at 8:00. / I get up at 8:00, usually.)

BE CAREFUL! Rarely and never are negative adverbs. Don't use them in affirmative sentences. (NOT: Yes, I rarely stay up late. → No, I rarely stay up late.)

5. Ever in Yes/No Questions: Use ever in yes/no questions to ask about frequency. Ever means "at any time or times." (e.g., A: Do you ever sleep late? B: Yes, often.)

BE CAREFUL! Don’t use ever in affirmative statements. (NOT: I ever sleep late. → I sometimes sleep late.)

6. How Often: Use how often questions to ask about frequency. (e.g., A: How often do you exercise? B: I usually exercise three times a week.)
